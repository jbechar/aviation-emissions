DATA SHEET // NOx

NOx (net, ~5%)

At cruise altitude, NOx speeds up ozone formation, a warming effect, but also raises hydroxyl radicals that break down methane, a cooling effect. The two nearly offset, leaving a small net warming, though the exact size is genuinely debated.

DATA SHEET // CONTRAIL CIRRUS

Contrail cirrus (~72%)

Water vapour in engine exhaust freezes onto soot particles at cruise altitude, forming ice crystal trails that can spread into thin cirrus cloud cover. Like natural cirrus, they let sunlight through but trap outgoing heat, giving a net warming effect.

The 72% figure is a fleet-wide average: Teoh et al. (2020, 2024) find that roughly 2-3% of flights, mostly long-haul, high-altitude, night-time routes, drive about 80% of total contrail warming.

DATA SHEET // CO2

CO2 (~22%)

Burning jet fuel releases CO2, which traps outgoing heat the way a blanket traps body heat. It persists in the atmosphere for centuries, so its warming effect accumulates and lasts long after the flight itself.
